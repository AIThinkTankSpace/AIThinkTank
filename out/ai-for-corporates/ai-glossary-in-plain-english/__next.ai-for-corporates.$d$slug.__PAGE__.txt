3:T30f2,<p>Your boss in the meeting: "We need to think about the implications of LLMs and fine-tuning our model using better training data to reduce hallucinations while optimizing the tokenization..."</p>
<p>You, internally: What?</p>
<p>Here's every AI term you've heard in meetings, explained so it actually makes sense.</p>
<hr>
<h2>The Core Concepts</h2>
<h3>1. AI (Artificial Intelligence)</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> A computer system that can do things that normally require human thinking.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> It's like asking someone very smart to do a task, except the "someone" is a computer program.</p>
<p><strong>Why your boss mentions it:</strong> When they say "we need an AI strategy," they mean "we need technology that thinks, not just calculates."</p>
<hr>
<h3>2. Machine Learning</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> AI systems that improve themselves by learning from examples instead of being explicitly programmed.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> You learn to recognize dogs by seeing many dogs, not by reading a 1,000-page manual describing every dog. Machine learning is the same—the computer learns by example.</p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong> A spam filter that blocks bad emails. You don't program "if contains 'free money' = spam." Instead, you show it 10,000 examples of spam and good emails. It learns the pattern.</p>
<hr>
<h3>3. Deep Learning</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> A type of machine learning that uses multiple layers of pattern-recognition, inspired by how your brain works.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> If machine learning is learning by examples, deep learning is learning by finding patterns within patterns. It can understand more complex things.</p>
<p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> This is what powers most modern AI—ChatGPT, image recognition, self-driving cars.</p>
<hr>
<h3>4. Neural Network</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> A computer structure inspired by how neurons in your brain connect. It processes information through many layers of connections.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> Your brain has neurons that connect to each other. Neural networks copy that structure digitally. Information comes in, gets processed through many layers, and comes out as an answer.</p>
<p><strong>Why it sounds scary:</strong> It's not. It's just a structure. Like saying "the building uses a steel frame"—it's just how it's built.</p>
<hr>
<h2>Language and Models</h2>
<h3>5. LLM (Large Language Model)</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> A huge neural network trained on billions of words that can predict and generate human language.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> Imagine reading every book, article, and website ever written, then becoming so good at understanding patterns that you can predict what word comes next. That's an LLM.</p>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong> ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini—all LLMs.</p>
<hr>
<h3>6. GPT</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> Stands for "Generative Pre-trained Transformer." It's the architecture that OpenAI uses for ChatGPT.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> "Generative" = creates stuff (writes text, generates images). "Pre-trained" = already trained on lots of data. "Transformer" = the architecture it uses.</p>
<p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> It's the specific type of neural network that's great at language understanding.</p>
<hr>
<h3>7. NLP (Natural Language Processing)</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> Teaching computers to understand and work with human language (not computer code).</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> Your email app understanding that "Can you call me?" is a request, not a question. That's NLP.</p>
<p><strong>Why it's important:</strong> Every AI system that understands English, Hindi, or any human language uses NLP.</p>
<hr>
<h3>8. Chatbot</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> An AI program that has conversations with humans.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> A robot receptionist that can answer questions and have basic conversations.</p>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong> ChatGPT, customer service bots, Siri, Alexa.</p>
<hr>
<h2>Training and Data</h2>
<h3>9. Training Data</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> The examples a machine learning system learns from.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> If you wanted to teach a child what fruit is, you'd show them 100 examples of apples, oranges, bananas, etc. That's training data.</p>
<p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> Garbage in = garbage out. Better training data = smarter AI.</p>
<hr>
<h3>10. Fine-tuning</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> Taking an AI model that's already trained and teaching it specific new information for a specific use case.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> You know English, but you want to specialize in medical terminology. So you take a focused course on medical language. That's fine-tuning.</p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong> OpenAI trained ChatGPT on general internet text, then fine-tuned it to be helpful, harmless, and honest through special training.</p>
<hr>
<h3>11. Token</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> A small chunk of text (usually 4 characters, roughly 1 word) that the AI processes.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> You're reading a book. The AI doesn't read whole pages at once—it reads small pieces called tokens, one by one.</p>
<p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> When you see "tokens used: 2,500" in ChatGPT, that means it processed about 2,500 words/units.</p>
<p><strong>Practical note:</strong> Longer conversations use more tokens, which costs more money (usually).</p>
<hr>
<h3>12. Algorithm</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> A step-by-step process that a computer follows to solve a problem or make a decision.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> A recipe. "Mix flour, add sugar, bake at 350°F" is a step-by-step process. An algorithm is the same thing for computers.</p>
<p><strong>Why it's mentioned:</strong> When your boss says "the algorithm decided," they mean the computer program followed its coded instructions.</p>
<hr>
<h2>Generative AI and Capabilities</h2>
<h3>13. Generative AI</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> AI that creates new things (text, images, code, video) based on patterns it learned.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> You read a lot of romance novels, so you could write your own romance novel in a similar style. Generative AI is the same—it learned patterns and can generate new stuff.</p>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong> ChatGPT generates text. DALL-E generates images. Both are generative AI.</p>
<hr>
<h3>14. Computer Vision</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> Teaching computers to understand and analyze images and video.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> Giving a computer eyes. It can now "see" and understand what's in images.</p>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong> Facial recognition, object detection in photos, medical imaging analysis.</p>
<hr>
<h3>15. Hallucination</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> When an AI confidently produces false information that sounds real.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> Someone tells you something with total confidence, but it's completely made up. They're hallucinating.</p>
<p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> ChatGPT sometimes "hallucinates" and makes up facts, citations, or statistics. It sounds right but is wrong. You can't just trust AI outputs blindly.</p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong> You ask ChatGPT for a study about cats. It confidently cites a fake study by a fake researcher. It's a hallucination.</p>
<hr>
<h2>Operations and Architecture</h2>
<h3>16. API (Application Programming Interface)</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> A way for different software to talk to each other and share information.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> A waiter in a restaurant. Your table (program A) asks the waiter (API) for something, and the waiter brings it from the kitchen (program B). The waiter is the interface between you and the kitchen.</p>
<p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> Companies integrate AI into their apps via APIs—your app talks to the AI system without needing to build the AI itself.</p>
<hr>
<h3>17. Bias</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> When an AI system is systematically unfair or wrong for certain groups of people.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> If you only learned about cooking from French cuisine, you'd have a bias—you'd think that's all cooking is.</p>
<p><strong>Real AI example:</strong> A hiring AI trained on data where men were hired more often will bias toward recommending male candidates, even though gender shouldn't matter.</p>
<p><strong>Why it's important:</strong> Biased AI can discriminate. Companies spend time removing bias.</p>
<hr>
<h3>18. Automation</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> Using AI or software to handle tasks that humans used to do manually.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> A factory replacing human workers with robots that do the same job 24/7.</p>
<p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> AI can automate routine work—sending emails, processing documents, analyzing data, customer service.</p>
<hr>
<h2>Advanced Concepts</h2>
<h3>19. Prompt</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> The instructions or questions you give to an AI system to get a response.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> What you say to ask someone for help. "Can you explain machine learning?" is a prompt.</p>
<p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> A good prompt gets better answers. "Explain machine learning" vs. "Explain machine learning in simple terms for someone with no technical background"—the second is a better prompt.</p>
<hr>
<h3>20. AGI (Artificial General Intelligence)</h3>
<p><strong>In one sentence:</strong> Hypothetical AI that can do anything a human can do, across any field.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world analogy:</strong> A superintelligent AI that's good at everything—coding, surgery, teaching, art, strategy.</p>
<p><strong>Reality check:</strong> We don't have AGI yet. Current AI (including ChatGPT) is narrow—good at specific things, not everything.</p>
<p><strong>Why your boss mentions it:</strong> Sometimes in strategy meetings about the future. Most of what exists now is narrow AI, not general.</p>
